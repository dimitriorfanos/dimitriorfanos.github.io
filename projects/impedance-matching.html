im·ped·ance
/ ɪmˈpiːd(ə)ns /
noun  ·  electrical engineering
  1. 1. Impedance is the total opposition a circuit or component offers to the flow of alternating current (AC). Measured in ohms (Ω) and represented by the symbol Z, it is the AC equivalent of electrical resistance.
  2. 2. In transmission line and waveguide systems, the ratio of the complex voltage to complex current at a given point — determining how much of an incident wave is reflected at a discontinuity. "Maximum power transfer occurs when the source impedance is the complex conjugate of the load impedance."
Related: reflection coefficient  ·  VSWR  ·  Smith chart  ·  stub tuner  ·  S-parameters

Impedance Matching Interest

During pyrolysis testing, we observed drift in the tuner position over time which would eventually lead to extinguishing of our plasma or disturbance to our process. We didn't have a clear picture of what the tuner output was actually doing to the impedance environment between the tuner and the reactor.

That gap was the starting point for this project. The 4-stub tuner we use isn't well-documented in the open literature — unlike the 3-stub tuner, which has published models and matchable-area analysis from S-TEAM and academic theses. I wanted to understand what the X and Y position numbers actually mean in terms of reflection coefficient magnitude and phase at the tuner output. Going deeper wasn't purely academic: if tuner position encodes something about plasma coupling and reactor stability, it's a diagnostic — and that's directly useful for understanding what was changing during those failed pyrolysis runs.

Simulation and modeling process

The modeling approach followed a two-stage process: validate against known literature first, then extend to the undocumented hardware. I started by re-creating a 3-stub tuner in CST Studio Suite (Learning Edition) using published dimensions and S-parameter data from Li Song's PhD thesis (University of Regina) and Vladimir Bilik's S-TEAM impedance matching literature as benchmarks. The 3-stub model uses WR340 waveguide (86.36 mm × 43.18 mm) at 2.45 GHz, with stub diameter 20 mm and λg/4 spacing of 43.14 mm. The tuner length is one guided wavelength (173.2 mm) at the operating frequency. Once the simulation reproduced the published S11 and S12 magnitude/phase curves within visual agreement across all three stub positions, I applied the same geometry and solver settings to the 4-stub tuner using physical measurements taken directly from the hardware — the manufacturer does not publish detailed CAD or dimensions.

With the 4-stub model built, I ran a coarse position sweep from (10,10) to (90,90) in steps of 10 — 81 cases — to map the full S22 coverage on the polar plane. To make the data suitable for interpolation, I followed up with a fine sweep at 1-position increments across the same range, producing 6561 simulation cases. The S22 data for each case was exported from CST and compiled into a single spreadsheet, then processed in Python using scipy's griddata to generate a smooth interpolation surface over the (X, Y) tuner position space. This surface allows non-integer tuner positions from live experimental logs to be converted to estimated S22 magnitude and phase without re-running the simulation.

CST model description

The CST model is a purely electromagnetic simulation — no plasma, no thermal effects — using the time-domain transient solver to extract broadband S-parameters around 2.45 GHz. The model geometry captures the waveguide body and stub cylinders as lossless copper conductors, with Port 1 defined at the magnetron-facing end and Port 2 at the reactor-facing end. No boundary condition constraining S11 to zero is applied, so both ports see reflections freely. Key model parameters:

Parameter Value / Description
Waveguide WR340 — 86.36 mm × 43.18 mm; copper (annealed), lossy metal material
Stub geometry 20 mm diameter cylinders; 3-stub spacing 43.14 mm center-to-center; 4-stub spacing ~22 mm with X-group (stubs 1 & 2) and Y-group (stubs 3 & 4) pairs separated by 58 mm; sweep range 10–90 position units (≈ 6–22 mm insertion depth)
Port setup Waveguide ports with fundamental TE₁₀ mode excitation; frequency range 2.2–2.7 GHz
Mesh Adaptive auto-mesh with convergence monitor on S11 magnitude; results evaluated at 2.45 GHz and 2.44 GHz
Post-processing S22 magnitude and phase extracted at 2.45 GHz per (X, Y) case via result template; exported to CSV and interpolated externally

Lessons learned

01
Home position (50, 50) introduces no impedance transformation

The 4-stub tuner at (50, 50) is equivalent to the 3-stub tuner at (0, 0, 0) — a plain waveguide with no stubs inserted. It introduces near-zero S22 magnitude, meaning no standing wave between the tuner and load. Euclidean distance from (50, 50) shows a strong positive correlation with S22 magnitude: the further the stub position from home, the higher the output reflection coefficient.

02
S22 magnitude increases with distance from (50, 50)

Plotting S22 magnitude against Euclidean distance from (50, 50) across all 6561 simulation cases reveals a consistent positive trend. Stub positions at the extremes (near 10 or 90) produce high reflection coefficients and high SWR between the tuner output and the load — a useful operational heuristic for interpreting automatic tuner behavior.

03
4-stub design eliminates the stub swapping discontinuity of 3-stub tuners

In a 3-stub tuner, matching a load that crosses between impedance regions requires stubs 1 and 3 to swap dramatically — a physical discontinuity that can cause transient instability. The 4-stub design avoids this by providing continuous coverage across the Smith chart. For automatic tuners with fast mechanical response, stub swapping is less critical, but the 4-stub geometry handles dynamic loads more gracefully.

04
Simulation data enables post-processing of experimental tuner logs

Mapping logged stub positions to the interpolated S22 surface reveals how impedance evolves over the course of a run. In experiments where the tuner operated in automatic mode and kept reflected power below 1%, the S22 phase remained relatively stable while the magnitude trended upward — indicating a gradual change in the load seen by the tuner and providing a basis for investigating process stability and duration.
